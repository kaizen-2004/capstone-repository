# Raspberry Pi - Local Studies

Gonzales et al. (2025) show how to use an ESP32-CAM to track buses and seats in real time with an IoT and computer vision system. The architecture combines GPS, FOMO object detection based on a microcontroller, and a mobile application backend. This shows that ESP32 devices can do real-time analytics in environments with limited resources using lightweight algorithms. This proves that ESP32 can be used as a front-end sensor and vision platform for data pipelines that need to be processed quickly. It also gives a good example of how to design real-time embedded systems. This study is relevant because it shows how microcontroller-based edge devices can reliably capture and send sensor or visual data for further processing. This supports the ESP32's role in fire detection and sensor integration. Local studies reveal the implementation of the ESP3 in home security. An intrusion alarm system integrated PIR motion sensing, magnetic door detection, a buzzer, a camera, and GSM-based SMS alerts. The test results showed that the detection of unauthorized entry was always done accurately and the notification delivery was also very stable, thus proving the capability of ESP32 in the field of automated home safety applications (Dequiros et al., 2022).
Another local researcher has employed ESP32 to build a medical monitoring system. A flex-sensor belt was used to measure chest and abdominal movement for respiratory rate monitoring, and the real-time data was displayed on the OLED and sent to the web server. The overall accuracy reached 93.73 percent, and the results were even better when the sensor was placed on the abdomen. The study confirms that ESP32 can be an efficient wireless health monitoring platform (Mamado et al., 2024).
The majority of early research on environmental monitoring systems relied on fully centralized hardware, which combined data storage, computation, and sensing into a single device. Although these systems operated dependably in steady industrial environments, they were inherently fragile due to their closely coupled architecture, which caused the entire monitoring chain to fail when the core unit failed. Researchers gradually shifted to distributed sensing utilizing platforms like Arduino and early ESP boards when more reasonably priced microcontrollers became available. This shift boosted deployment flexibility and decreased costs, but it also brought to light real-world challenges, especially with regard to scheduling sampling, sustaining wireless connectivity, and integrating devices with new cloud infrastructures. A more decoupled paradigm where lightweight sensor nodes publish directly to cloud services was promoted by the emergence of modern IoT platforms like AWS IoT Core, Azure IoT Hub, and Blynk. Because of its integrated wireless capabilities and compatibility with a wide range of digital environmental sensors, such as SHT humidity modules, BMP pressure sensors, and VOC and CO₂ monitors, the ESP32 garnered a lot of attention in this context.
Nevertheless, a large portion of the literature continued to concentrate on single-device prototypes, meaning that the difficulties of managing asynchronous data streams or synchronizing many field nodes were hardly ever discussed. Similar trends were seen in research on the Raspberry Pi, which was mostly treated as a straightforward gateway rather than an analytical master node. Thapa and K. C.'s work, on the other hand, closes this gap by presenting a low-cost multi-node system in which data from two ESP32 field devices is fed to a Raspberry Pi, which synchronizes, averages, and transmits the results to the cloud for visualization. Their findings demonstrate that low-cost technology may provide dependable, real-time environmental monitoring in real-world scenarios when structured inside a carefully considered architecture.
