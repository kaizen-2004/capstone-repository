# Real-time Detection - Foreign Studies

Recent advances in Artificial Intelligence and the Internet of Things (IoT) have improved real-time detection systems compared with earlier single-modality approaches. Panindre et al. (2025) showed that combining Scaled-YOLOv4 with EfficientDet-D2 can improve fire monitoring accuracy on live CCTV feeds. However, vision-only pipelines remain vulnerable to false alarms when no independent sensor evidence is available.

Saranya et al. (2020) demonstrated real-time intruder detection using deep learning and multi-model recognition, but they still reported challenges under occlusion, unusual angles, and low-light conditions. These limitations are relevant to residential monitoring, where visibility and scene quality vary over time.

Cost-focused studies by Iqbal et al. (2021), Okokpujie et al. (2023), and Jyothi et al. (2016) showed that low-cost platforms (e.g., Raspberry Pi, ESP32, and related edge devices) can support practical monitoring deployments. A recurring gap across these studies is limited multi-evidence verification, where systems rely heavily on motion cues or isolated image snapshots.

Taken together, foreign studies support the need for integrated real-time architectures that combine visual analysis with non-visual evidence to improve reliability and reduce false alarms in low-light and dynamic environments.
