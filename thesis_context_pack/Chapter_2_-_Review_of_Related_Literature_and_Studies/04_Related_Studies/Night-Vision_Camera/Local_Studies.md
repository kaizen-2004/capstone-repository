# Night-Vision Camera - Local Studies

Actual system advancements in the Philippines illustrate the practical application of night-vision concepts. Abaya et al. (2017) constructed an economical night-vision security camera utilizing a Raspberry Pi and an infrared module, demonstrating that basic embedded systems are capable of performing night-vision functions. A subsequent assessment by APCoRE (2023) evaluated night-vision CCTV cameras installed on a local campus, yielding empirical performance data regarding visibility and clarity in nocturnal conditions.
Abaya et al. (2016) previously integrated PIR sensors with infrared lights to initiate image capture solely upon motion detection. This demonstrated that night-vision systems can conserve energy while ensuring surveillance. Batinggal and Nigidula (2018) also used low-light video enhancement techniques to show how processing steps that are similar to night-vision preprocessing can make things easier to see.
There have also been a number of local uses for infrared sensing. Cruz et al. (2020) created an infrared thermal-imaging device that can take pictures without visible light. This shows that IR sensors are a good choice for tasks that need to be done at night. Velasco (2021) counted the number of people on a bus using an infrared camera during the day and at night. De Leon (2024) also showed that an infrared camera on a Raspberry Pi can see things moving in low-light places, like a water flume, in this case tomatoes.
Llorente (2018) and Llorente et al. (2019) looked into ways to use light in their studies. These studies compared white-LED and infrared lighting in low-light situations and showed that controlled lighting, like dimmable LEDs, can help people find and follow others in the dark.
