The real-time detection systems on the edge that use the Raspberry Pi or the ESP32 series of microcontrollers have evolved significantly, as per the comprehensive review of the literature. Most of the studies have shown that relatively simple convolutional models can now handle challenging computer-vision tasks such as facial recognition or flame detection on limited hardware. At the same time, some other works have looked into sensor fusion methods that take in the visual, thermal, and gas data to improve the precision. Although these works serve as valuable reference points, they also draw attention to the problems that are still prevalent in the industry, particularly in the areas of integrated system design, false-alarm reduction, night-time operation, and standard evaluation of edge architectures. The matrix that has been uploaded reveals that many of the flame-detection pipelines are still largely dependent on visible-light cues, which results in a sharp decrease in performance when the lighting conditions are poor. Likewise, several facial-recognition methods are affected by occlusion, smoke, and lack of light. The synergistic advantages of different sensing methods are not fully exploited by the disintegrated systems resulting from other studies that consider ambient sensor monitoring and vision-based detection as separate fields. There are also instances of unstable latency and lowered frame rate on ESP32-based solutions, which point to insufficient understanding of the trade-offs between real-time performance, hardware resource allocation, and model complexity. Moreover, only a handful of papers offer stringent, reproducible benchmarks that compare the performance of single-device and hybrid embedded architectures, and the literature on this subject also mentions that systems employing only color or rule-based cues tend to have high false-alarm rates, especially in dynamic indoor situations.
This project puts a stop to such limitations by developing a multimodal, integrated detection system that combines environmental sensing, flame detection, and facial recognition into a single edge pipeline. The system uses ESP32-CAM and ESP32-C3 nodes for distributed acquisition, Raspberry Pi 2 for local server hosting, and a second-hand laptop for vision inference, building a cohesive split-host architecture that leverages the strengths of each device class. The occlusion-tolerant facial recognition workflow is supported by IR-assisted night operation to reduce low-light degradation. The study also introduces an adaptive fusion technique that reduces false alarms, which are most frequent in vision-only or sensor-only systems, by verifying optical detections against smoke and door-force evidence. Contrary to most of the previous works, this research will also furnish quantitative benchmarks that compare energy consumption, frame rate, accuracy, and latency in different conditions. The emphasis on systematic assessment is intended to overcome the obvious lack of thorough performance evaluation in the previous works.
The research combines unified sensing, improved robustness under challenging conditions, and systematic performance evaluation to present a more reliable and practical approach to real-time detection on low-cost embedded hardware. It aims to prove that a well-coordinated multimodal architecture can deliver consistent detection performance in high-risk scenarios, such as occlusions and night, and can outperform the isolated methods cited by the literature. Consequently, it positions itself as a direct answer to the voids left by the previous research and puts forward a framework that can be further developed and expanded by embedded detection systems going forward.
