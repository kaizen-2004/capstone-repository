# Raspberry Pi - Local Literature

Philippine literature highlights the growing adoption of microcontrollers and single-board computers among students and hobbyists. Circuit Rocks (2025) compares Arduino, ESP32, and Raspberry Pi, identifying ESP32 as cost-effective for wireless sensor systems and Raspberry Pi as suitable for projects requiring higher processing power or dashboards. Keep Booming Tech (2025) emphasizes the Raspberry Pi Pico W’s dual-core performance, native Wi-Fi, and flexible programming, making it ideal for IoT and STEM projects. These sources, however, focus on single-device applications, revealing a lack of local studies integrating ESP32 and Raspberry Pi in unified real-time systems.
Internationally, Raspberry Pi is widely applied as a gateway or edge processor in domains like automation, health monitoring, and industrial control (Computer Science Review, 2024), complementing ESP32’s role in data capture and wireless transmission. Kadhim et al. (2023) demonstrate a wearable system for Alzheimer’s patients combining ESP32-CAM and Raspberry Pi for facial recognition and real-time audio feedback, exemplifying effective integration of low-power microcontrollers and SBCs for time-sensitive tasks. Gonzales et al. (2025) illustrate ESP32-based real-time bus tracking using FOMO object detection, confirming the microcontroller’s suitability for edge analytics, though Raspberry Pi was not included.
The literature indicates that while ESP32 and Raspberry Pi are individually supported for IoT and real-time applications, there is limited Philippine research on their integrated deployment. This study addresses that gap by designing a real-time intruder and fire monitoring alert system that uses ESP32-based nodes for sensing, local monitoring software for alerting and logging, and camera-based computer vision for face recognition and fire-related visual confirmation. Raspberry Pi 2 is retained as an optional deployment component for local edge hosting, contributing to unified, time-sensitive IoT applications locally.
The local research on environmental monitoring through technological means reveals that both Arduino and Raspberry Pi are capable of delivering inexpensive solutions for the measurement of particulates and gases in the air. The two microcontroller systems can present air quality data in real-time and at the same time, they can make the deployment process affordable for the communities and schools (Sabado, 2024).
