# Flame Detection - Local Literature

Local studies on fire detection emphasize the use of multi-sensor systems to address the limitations of single-modality detectors. Malbog et al. (2020) developed a rule-based household fire detection system integrating gas, smoke, and flame sensors with SMS alerts. Their findings showed that combining multiple sensors significantly reduced false alarms compared to vision-based or single-sensor systems, particularly under varying lighting conditions. Although the system lacked spatial awareness, it demonstrated that multi-sensor fusion improves reliability in residential settings.
Extending this approach, Elizalde et al. (2018) applied multi-sensor fire detection to outdoor environments by incorporating temperature and ionization smoke sensors with GSM and GPS technologies. Their work shifted the focus from detection accuracy alone to communication range, fault tolerance, and real-time location reporting, highlighting the importance of actionable emergency response. Similarly, Tagadiad and Perin (2021) proposed a microcontroller-based system combining flame, smoke, and temperature sensors for home use, achieving stable performance despite visual obstructions and lighting changes.
More advanced decision-making was introduced by Sedillo et al. (2023), who employed fuzzy logic to interpret temperature, gas, smoke, and carbon monoxide sensor data. This approach allowed fire risk to be assessed gradually rather than as a binary condition, reducing ambiguity caused by borderline sensor readings. Baba et al. (2022) further reinforced the effectiveness of IoT-based multi-sensor systems by integrating multiple environmental sensors with wireless communication for low-cost residential and small-business applications.
