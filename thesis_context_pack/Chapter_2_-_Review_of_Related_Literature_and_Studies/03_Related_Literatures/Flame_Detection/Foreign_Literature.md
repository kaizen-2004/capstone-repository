# Flame Detection - Foreign Literature

Early video-based flame detection studies relied primarily on color-based heuristics. Chen, Wu, and Chiou (2004) demonstrated that simple chromatic rules, such as higher red-channel intensity, are insufficient for reliable detection because non-flame objects often share similar color properties. Their work emphasized the importance of incorporating temporal features, including flicker and shape variation, establishing fire as a dynamic visual phenomenon rather than a static object.
Spectral-based detection was explored by Oshima et al. (2009), who used a solar-blind ultraviolet (UV) sensor to detect UV-C emissions unique to real flames. This approach achieved high reliability in low-visibility and nighttime conditions with minimal false alarms. However, because the sensor lacked imaging capability, it could not provide spatial information such as flame location or size, limiting its standalone applicability in surveillance systems.
To improve robustness, Bohush and Brouka (2013) introduced a multi-feature fusion approach combining color, motion, temporal flicker, and texture features. Their method demonstrated improved accuracy in complex environments and enabled both fire and smoke detection. Despite its effectiveness, the approach required higher computational resources and was sensitive to lighting variations due to reliance on handcrafted features.
Ri et al. (2018) addressed computational constraints by proposing a real-time flame detection method suitable for CCTV and embedded systems. By combining color segmentation, motion analysis, and wavelet-based temporal features, their approach achieved efficient performance with reduced feature complexity. Nevertheless, the system remained vulnerable to illumination changes and low-light conditions.
Recent studies, such as Obayuwana et al. (2024), reflect the shift toward deep-learning-based and IoT-enabled flame detection systems. Their framework employed lightweight convolutional neural networks and emphasized the importance of diverse training datasets to improve real-world performance. While these models offer improved adaptability and scalability, challenges persist when operating across varying lighting conditions.
