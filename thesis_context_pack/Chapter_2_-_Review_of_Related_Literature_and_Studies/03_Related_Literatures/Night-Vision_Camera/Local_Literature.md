# Night-Vision Camera - Local Literature

Normal cameras have trouble when the light level drops significantly, according to USJR's (2019) investigation into the effect of altered light intensity on normal camera performance. For this reason, in order to maintain clear images, night vision cameras need more light or specific sensors. The local subject of infrared thermal behavior in the tropics was studied by De Asis et al. (2021). Their results indicate that heat sources can be identified even when there is no visible light present. This supports local night vision systems' use of infrared sensors.
By improving the performance of CCTV footage captured in low light, Gonzales et al. (2018) also helped to advance low-light imaging. Their presentation raises questions about how procedures that would have been concealed otherwise can be improved to provide details. In a distinct local study, Hisuan (2016) examined the various techniques for imaging in low light and noted that standard cameras frequently yield poor quality images, highlighting the importance of night vision technology development.
Valdez et al. (2020) brought up the issue of poorly illuminated security cameras in Philippine cities as one of the primary issues that most negatively impacts their effectiveness. They went on to say that normal cameras' sensors are less effective at night since the video they record is so hazy. They underlined how crucial infrared-assisted units or at the very least, more lighting are to improving the area's nighttime security.
A study on the effectiveness of using imaging systems using infrared technology in community watch zones was carried out by Reyes et al. in 2022. Their research revealed that the creation and application of infrared LEDs significantly improve object visibility at night, especially in places where street light is scarce. As a result, this technology can be used for night vision in many different contexts.
Mendoza et al. (2017) investigated how well cameras installed outside in rural barangays performed in different illumination scenarios. According to their poll, it is quite difficult to acquire a clear picture when there is no natural light present, particularly if you want to observe a moving item or a face. This has led to a request for additional units that address the issue of darkness in the visual field.
An investigation into the usage of imaging technology during emergency rescue operations at night was carried out by Santiago and Ramos (2019). They said that because thermal and infrared imaging significantly improve visibility in places that are dark or smoke-filled, they have emerged as the most widely used technology for disaster response in the Philippines.
The efficacy of using near-infrared cameras in university security systems was examined by Cabrera et al. (2023). Their research showed that NIR-based imaging may provide a respectable level of clarity even in almost complete darkness, hence offering the advantages of using night-vision-adjacent technology in educational institutions.
